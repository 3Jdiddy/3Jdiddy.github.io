Overview

Kofun Period ~300-550 AD

The Kofun period of Japan is characterized by, and obtains its namesake from, the megalithic keyhole-shaped tombs known as Kofun that were constructed in the Japanese archipelago from the middle of the 3rd century to the early 7th century AD.


Within these tombs, ancient figures crafted for ritual use called Haniwa could be found. These terracotta clay figures displayed clothing, hairstyles, tools, and architecture used at the time. Therefore, they serve as historical archives of the Kofun period.


The Kofun period also recorded Japan's earliest political centralization, when the Yamato clan rose to power in southwestern Japan and established the Imperial House, facilitating trade routes across the region.

So What?

Historical Importance


Aside from the Hinawa serving as important primary sources-- granting insight into the culture of the Japanese people of the time-- the Kofun period, and the changes in art culture in the centuries that followed, are unique and potent examples of the power of cultural influence.


Specifically, I believe examining the cultural influence China had on Japan in the centuries begining in the Kofun period-- with the Kofun period acting as a starting point of the encroachment of culture's from China and the southern Korean Peninsula-- grants immense insight into how the identity of cultures can shift due to cultural influence. The understanding of this phenomina is relevant to all areas of historical studies-- regardless of the region in question.
